The Big Story

Iran Names Interim Leader as Bombs Fall — Arafi Steps In, Succession Clock Ticking

What’s going on:

Iran named senior cleric Ayatollah Alireza Arafi as jurist member of its Leadership Council on Sunday, tasked with fulfilling Supreme Leader duties until a permanent successor is elected by the Assembly of Experts. It is an emergency appointment in the middle of an active war: Israeli airstrikes continued hammering Tehran throughout Sunday morning, with Israeli forces declaring they were targeting the “heart” of the city. Iran’s military continues to fire back — Qatar, Kuwait, and Bahrain intercepting missiles; Oman’s key US Navy logistics hub at Duqm hit by drones; a tanker in the Strait of Hormuz attacked. Protests turned deadly in Karachi, where 9 were killed storming the US Consulate.

The takeaway:

Naming an interim leader while bombs are still falling is itself a signal: Iran’s clerical establishment is attempting to project continuity, not collapse. But Arafi is a consensus placeholder, not a commanding figure. The real succession fight — between Mojtaba Khamenei (the dead leader’s son), reformist clerics, and hardliners — will play out in the Assembly of Experts over weeks or months. Meanwhile the war goes on. And every day it continues, the Hormuz disruption grows more economically catastrophic. This is no longer just a military story; it is a global energy and political crisis unfolding in real time.

Oil Jumps 10% — Analysts Say $92–$100+ Is Base Case If Hormuz Stays Shut

Crude oil surged 10% Sunday as markets digested the Hormuz closure. Rystad Energy expects prices to settle around $92 a barrel near-term, with analysts warning of $100+ if disruptions persist. OPEC+ agreed to a modest 206,000 bpd output increase — roughly 1% of the Hormuz daily flow — which analysts called “symbolic at best.” Gold also hit multi-month highs as safe-haven demand spiked.

What it means: This is not a blip — it’s a structural price shift until the strait reopens. At $92–$100 oil, US gas prices could climb well above $3.50/gallon. Airlines are repricing. The Fed’s rate path just got more complicated. Every central bank is running new inflation scenarios today.

And Finally…

A Fish That Survived the Dinosaurs Gets Its Close-Up

What’s going on:

In considerably better news from the deep: French divers have captured the first-ever full video sequence of an Indonesian coelacanth in its natural habitat. The coelacanth — a fish that was thought to be extinct for 65 million years until one turned up in a South African fish market in 1938 — has never before been filmed at depth by humans in Indonesian waters. This was not a blurry sonar ping or a half-seen shadow. It was a proper, extended video sequence of a creature that has been swimming around largely unchanged since before the age of dinosaurs.

The takeaway:

The ocean is very deep and still full of surprises. While geopolitical fires burn above the surface, a team of French scientists quietly descended into the depths off Indonesia and came back with footage of a living fossil. The coelacanth does not care about your news cycle. It has seen empires rise and fall. It will be fine.
