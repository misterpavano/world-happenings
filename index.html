The Big Story

War Spreads to Lebanon — Hezbollah Opens Second Front as Gulf Burns and Hormuz Closes

What’s going on:

Day three of the U.S.-Israeli war on Iran arrived Monday with a new front: Hezbollah, the Iran-backed Lebanese militant group, began launching rockets and drones at northern Israel, breaking a 15-month ceasefire and drawing immediate Israeli airstrikes on Beirut’s southern suburbs. Israel’s military says it struck senior Hezbollah commanders and “dozens of command and control nodes,” with at least 31 killed in Lebanon so far. Meanwhile Iran’s retaliatory strikes across the Gulf escalated dramatically — ballistic missiles and drones hit not just U.S. military bases but civilian infrastructure in UAE, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Qatar. Iran struck Dubai in an apparent message to Gulf states that backed U.S. operations. And in a startling friendly-fire incident, Kuwaiti air defenses mistakenly shot down three U.S. Air Force F-15E Strike Eagles; all six airmen ejected and survived. On the Strait of Hormuz, tankers are now ablaze, major shipping firms have halted all transits, and vessel traffic has dropped 40–50% since Saturday. Iran has not formally ordered a closure but the effect is already nearly the same.

The takeaway:

The conflict has crossed two new thresholds in the past 24 hours: geography and alliance politics. Hezbollah’s entry pulls Lebanon back into a war it narrowly escaped 18 months ago — and dramatically widens the battlefield. Iran’s strikes on Dubai shattered the Gulf’s carefully maintained image of neutral commercial stability; those countries publicly warned against a U.S. strike and are now absorbing the blowback. The F-15 friendly-fire incident — six U.S. airmen ejecting over Kuwait because their own ally’s air defenses engaged them — underscores how fast coordination breaks down in a multi-theater war. And with Hormuz near-closed, the world is now watching a fuse that connects Middle East geopolitics to gas pumps in every country on earth. This is not a contained strike. It is a region in motion.

But it also risks drawing Lebanon back into catastrophic destruction. The next 48 hours will show whether this is a signaling strike or the start of a sustained campaign. Europe’s call for Hezbollah restraint landed somewhere between useless and ironic.

Congress Mobilizes on War Powers — Trump Warns Fight Could Last “Weeks,” Signals Openness to New Iranian Leadership

Senator Tim Kaine is forcing a Senate floor vote on the Iran war under the War Powers Act, with House Democrats planning an emergency resolution requiring Congressional authorization before further strikes. Trump responded by warning the conflict could last “weeks” and insisted the action was lawful under executive authority. He also signaled he was “open to talking” to whoever emerges as Iran’s new leadership — while simultaneously posting that Khamenei’s death was “Justice.” A few Republicans have joined the war powers push, but GOP leadership is resisting letting votes advance.

What it means: Trump started this war unilaterally, without Congressional notification in advance, and he knows a War Powers vote — even one that fails — creates political and legal pressure. The fact that Kaine is forcing a vote rather than holding hearings suggests Democrats believe the urgency is real. Meanwhile Trump’s “open to talks” signal, delivered mid-strike, is either a genuine off-ramp offer or a way to appear reasonable while the bombs continue to fall. The two statements are not obviously compatible.

Oil Surges to $82/Barrel, Stocks Slide — Hormuz Crisis Rewrites Every Economic Forecast

Brent crude hit $82.37 at Monday’s open — up nearly 10% — while U.S. WTI crude jumped to $73/barrel. European markets opened sharply lower; U.S. futures pointed negative. The immediate cause is simple: roughly 15 million barrels of oil transit the Strait of Hormuz every day, and that flow has now nearly stopped. OPEC+ agreed to raise output by 206,000 barrels per day, which analysts called “a symbolic gesture against a massive problem.” Gold surged on safe-haven demand. The dollar rallied.

What it means: Every global economic model changes today. At $82–$100 oil, U.S. gas prices will climb well above $3.50/gallon, possibly above $4. Airlines are repricing tickets. Shipping costs spike. Central banks that were close to cutting rates now face new inflation scenarios. The Fed had a policy meeting coming — it just got significantly more complicated. This is not a temporary shock; it’s a structural shift in energy prices for however long the strait stays disrupted.

FIFA Gave Trump a “Peace Prize” in December. Iran Was Supposed to Play in His World Cup in June.

What’s going on:

In December, at the grand 2026 FIFA World Cup draw ceremony, FIFA awarded Donald Trump its inaugural “Peace Prize,” saying he had “played a pivotal role” in establishing a tournament that would bridge nations. Iran qualified for the World Cup and was drawn into Group G, scheduled to play all three group-stage matches on U.S. soil — including a June 15 opener at SoFi Stadium in Los Angeles against New Zealand. On Monday, the head of Iran’s football federation said he had no idea whether the national team would actually show up. The Athletic and The Guardian both ran the story with varying degrees of visible struggle to keep a straight face.

The takeaway:

History will record that FIFA presented a peace prize to the man who, three months later, co-launched a war against the country scheduled to play in his World Cup. The tournament opens in 101 days. Iran’s Group G opponents — New Zealand, Spain, and Portugal — await clarity. FIFA has not commented on contingency plans. The organization has, historically, been very good at not commenting on geopolitics until forced to. They are about to be forced.
